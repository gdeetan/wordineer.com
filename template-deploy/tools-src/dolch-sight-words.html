      What are Dolch sight words?

      Dolch sight words are a list of high-frequency English words compiled by educator Edward William Dolch and published between 1936 and 1948. The list is divided into 220 service words — function words like pronouns, prepositions, and conjunctions that appear constantly in written English — grouped by grade level from Pre-K through 3rd grade, plus a supplementary list of common Dolch Nouns.

      The defining feature of a Dolch sight word is that it is taught by sight recognition rather than phonetic decoding. Many Dolch words are phonetically irregular ("said", "was", "they", "one") and cannot be sounded out reliably by early readers. Because these words appear so often in text — Dolch estimated that his 220 service words account for 50–75% of all words in typical children's books — fast, automatic recognition of them is essential for reading fluency.

      Dolch vs. Fry sight words: which list should you use?

      Both lists are widely used and neither is "better" in an absolute sense. The choice depends on your context:

      • Use Dolch if your school or curriculum explicitly references Dolch levels, if you want a grade-organized list, or if you're working with K-3 students.
      • Use Fry if you want frequency ordering, if you're working with readers above 3rd grade, or if you want to build toward the full 1,000-word list that covers through 9th grade.
      • Use both — many experienced teachers do. The lists overlap significantly (roughly 60% of Dolch words appear in Fry's first 300), so studying one reinforces the other.

      Dolch sight word teaching strategies

      Research on early literacy identifies several effective approaches for sight word instruction:

      • Flashcard repetition: Simple but effective. Show the word, child says it, confirm. Use saved-words to track which words need more repetitions.
      • Multisensory tracing: Have children trace the word in sand or on a rough surface while saying it. Kinesthetic engagement strengthens memory.
      • Context reading: Don't practice words in isolation only. Use short sentences where the sight word appears so children connect the word to meaning ("She said yes").
      • Daily short sessions: 5–10 minutes daily outperforms one longer weekly session. The Practice Mode in this tool is designed for exactly this — one quick run-through of a 10–20 word set.
      • Progress celebrations: When a child masters a grade group, mark the achievement. Motivation matters at this age.

      Frequently asked questions

      How many words are on the Dolch list?
      The Dolch list contains 315 words total: 220 service words grouped Pre-K through 3rd grade, and 95 Dolch Nouns. These words make up a very large share of words in early elementary reading material.
      What is the difference between the Dolch list and Fry list?
      Dolch (315 words, 1936–1948) is organized by grade level with an emphasis on function words. Fry (1,000 words, 1957–1980) is ordered purely by frequency and extends through Grade 9. Both are public domain. Most K-3 curricula use Dolch; Fry is used to track progress at higher grade levels.
      At what age should children know all the Dolch words?
      Most reading programs target Pre-K words by end of kindergarten, Kindergarten words by end of 1st grade, and the remaining service words (1st, 2nd, 3rd grade groups) by the end of 3rd grade. Children vary — these are targets, not strict deadlines.
      Can I use this tool to print Dolch flashcards?
      Yes. Filter to a grade group, generate the list, and click Print. The print view hides the navigation and shows only the word list. You can also click Copy List to paste the words into a flashcard app or word processor.
      Is Dr. Dolch's list still used in schools?
      Yes. Despite being developed in the 1930s–40s, the Dolch list remains a standard K-3 reading benchmark across the US because the function words it covers have not changed — "the," "and," "was," "said" are as common today as they were in 1948.
