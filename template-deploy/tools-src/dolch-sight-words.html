    What are Dolch sight words?

    Dolch sight words are a list of high-frequency English words compiled by educator Edward William Dolch and published between 1936 and 1948. The list is divided into 220 service words — function words like pronouns, prepositions, and conjunctions that appear constantly in written English — grouped by grade level from Pre-K through 3rd grade, plus a supplementary list of common Dolch Nouns.

    The defining feature of a Dolch sight word is that it is taught by sight recognition rather than phonetic decoding. Many Dolch words are phonetically irregular ("said", "was", "they", "one") and cannot be sounded out reliably by early readers. Because these words appear so often in text — Dolch estimated that his 220 service words account for 50–75% of all words in typical children's books — fast, automatic recognition of them is essential for reading fluency.

    Dolch vs. Fry sight words: which list should you use?

    Both lists are widely used and neither is "better" in an absolute sense. Use Dolch if your school or curriculum explicitly references Dolch levels, or if you're working with K-3 students. Use Fry if you want frequency ordering, or if you're working with readers above 3rd grade. The lists overlap significantly — roughly 60% of Dolch words appear in Fry's first 300 — so studying one reinforces the other. See the Fry Sight Words page for the frequency-ordered list.

    Dolch sight word teaching strategies

    Research on early literacy identifies several effective approaches: Flashcard repetition — show the word, child says it, confirm. Multisensory tracing — have children trace the word in sand while saying it. Context reading — use short sentences where the sight word appears so children connect the word to meaning. Daily short sessions — 5–10 minutes daily outperforms one longer weekly session.

    Frequently asked questions

    How many words are on the Dolch list?
    The Dolch list contains 315 words total: 220 service words grouped Pre-K through 3rd grade, and 95 Dolch Nouns. These words make up a very large share of words in early elementary reading material.
    What is the difference between the Dolch list and Fry list?
    Dolch (315 words, 1936–1948) is organized by grade level with an emphasis on function words. Fry (1,000 words, 1957–1980) is ordered purely by frequency and extends through Grade 9. Most K-3 curricula use Dolch; Fry is used to track progress at higher grade levels.
    At what age should children know all the Dolch words?
    Most reading programs target Pre-K words by end of kindergarten, Kindergarten words by end of 1st grade, and the remaining service words by the end of 3rd grade. These are targets, not strict deadlines — individual children vary.
    Can I use this tool to print Dolch flashcards?

    What does Practice Mode do?
    Practice Mode shows one word at a time. Click Hear Word for audio, Next to advance, and Prev to go back. Exit Practice returns to the full list. Designed for daily 5–10 minute drills.
    Is Dr. Dolch's list still used in schools?
    Yes. Despite being developed in the 1930s–40s, the Dolch list remains a K-3 standard because the function words it covers — "the," "and," "was," "said" — are as common today as they were in 1948.
