What is Gen Z slang?

Gen Z slang is the informal language used by and popularized among Generation Z — people born roughly between 1997 and 2012. Like every generation before them, Gen Z developed its own vocabulary to communicate identity, in-group belonging, irony, and emotion in ways that feel fresh and distinctly theirs. What separates this generation's slang from prior waves is not so much the mechanics of how slang works — that process is ancient — but the unprecedented speed at which new terms appear, spread globally, and burn out.

Before the internet, slang spread through physical communities: neighborhoods, schools, music scenes, subcultures. A term could simmer in one city for years before reaching another. Gen Z slang operates on a completely different timeline. A word coined in a TikTok video on a Monday can be in widespread use across three continents by Friday, and considered overused by the following month. The algorithm doesn't just distribute language — it accelerates its entire lifecycle.

This also means Gen Z slang is unusually diverse in its origins. Because the internet collapses geographic distance, Gen Z has absorbed vocabulary from Black American vernacular English, gaming communities, anime fandoms, K-pop stan culture, meme subcultures, and more, often mixing these registers in the same conversation. The result is a living, shifting lexicon that resists clean definition — which is exactly the point. Slang that everyone understands has already crossed into mainstream, and mainstream isn't cool anymore.

Where does Gen Z slang come from?

Tracing Gen Z slang to its sources reveals a surprisingly consistent pattern: most of it didn't start with Gen Z at all. The generation is a masterful remixer and amplifier of language from communities, subcultures, and media that existed long before them. Understanding those source pools makes the whole lexicon click into place.

Black American vernacular English (AAVE) is by far the largest single contributor. Words like slay, shade, tea, bussin, no cap, cap, lowkey, highkey, woke, and periodt all originate in Black American communities, many of them decades old in their original context. Slay as an expression of admiration was established in Black and drag culture well before social media amplified it globally. No cap is rooted in Black southern slang where capping means lying or boasting. The debt is significant, and worth naming plainly: Gen Z speaks largely in borrowed AAVE, and the borrowing often happens without credit given to its source communities.

Gaming communities contributed a distinct cluster of words. Sus exploded in 2020 via the multiplayer game Among Us, where it meant suspicious — a player behaving in a way that suggests they might be the impostor. GG (good game), no-scope, clutch, and npc (a person behaving with robotic predictability, borrowed from non-player character) all entered broader slang from gaming contexts. The gaming pipeline to general youth slang is reliable: gaming is where millions of young people spend hours every day, so language that develops there spreads quickly to everywhere else those young people are.

Stan culture and fandom communities gave rise to terms like stan itself (from the 2000 Eminem song about an obsessive fan, now meaning devoted superfan), ship (to want two people to be in a relationship), canon used in everyday conversation, and era as in "I'm in my villain era." These communities — organized around musicians, actors, athletes, and fictional characters — developed dense, efficient language for discussing parasocial relationships and group identity, and that language leaked into the mainstream.

TikTok is the single most powerful distribution mechanism for Gen Z slang in the current era. Terms like rizz (popularized by streamer Kai Cenat and spread via TikTok), delulu (from delusional, spread in stan spaces then TikTok), and countless audio-driven phrases all owe their reach to the platform's algorithm, which serves content to users based on engagement rather than social graph, making it possible for niche language to become global literally overnight.

Twitter/X and older meme culture contributed the irony-heavy, absurdist register that shows up in terms like ratio (when a reply gets more engagement than the original post, implying the original was wrong or bad), based (originally a Lil B term meaning unashamedly yourself, adopted by various online communities with shifting connotations), and the general sardonic tone that characterizes a lot of Gen Z humor.

How to use Gen Z slang without sounding awkward

The most reliable way to sound awkward using Gen Z slang is to use it just after you learned it from a news article, a parent guide, or a marketing deck. Slang exists partly as a social signal — knowing a term and using it naturally suggests you were already in the spaces where it circulates. Using it haltingly, or slightly wrong, signals the opposite: that you're an outsider trying to cross in.

The cardinal rule is: understand context before using. Slay used as a standalone exclamation works in contexts of genuine admiration. Saying "slay!" when someone presents a quarterly earnings report is incongruous in a way that reads as either oblivious or desperate. No cap works after a strong personal statement to underscore sincerity; dropped randomly mid-sentence, it loses that function and sounds like filler. Context is everything.

Understand what a word actually means before deploying it. Rizz isn't just confidence — it specifically implies effortless romantic magnetism. Using it to mean "general charisma in a business presentation" is technically wrong and will read as wrong to anyone fluent in the term. Based has an intentionally ambiguous edge — it can be a compliment or a subtle dig depending on who's saying it to whom. These nuances aren't decoration; they're the meaning.

Timing matters enormously. Gen Z slang has an accelerated obsolescence cycle. A term trending in 2023 may already feel dated in 2025 — especially if it was overexposed in brand advertising, news coverage, or used by politicians trying to seem relatable. This dictionary's status labels exist precisely for this reason: a term marked Fading or Retired is one to avoid if you're trying to sound current. If you're a parent trying to decode what your teenager said, retired terms are still useful — they may still be in active use in your household even if they've left the cutting edge.

The most honest approach: use slang you've genuinely absorbed, not slang you've studied. If you regularly spend time on TikTok, Discord, or gaming platforms, you'll pick up language naturally in context. If you're researching it externally, it's usually better to understand it than to attempt it.

Gen Z slang that went mainstream

One reliable signal that a Gen Z term has truly crossed over: it appears in a national newspaper headline, a corporate tweet, or a presidential campaign. At that point, the slang's social function within youth culture is largely exhausted — it's no longer a marker of in-group membership because everyone is in the group now.

FOMO (fear of missing out) made that crossing cleanly enough that it's now in most major dictionaries and is used across all age groups without irony. Ghosting — ending a relationship by simply going silent — became the word therapists, journalists, and dating columnists all reached for because no other single word captured the phenomenon as precisely. It crossed and stayed crossed. Woke began as Black activist language describing racial awareness, was adopted by progressive communities, and then became an ideological flashpoint when conservatives adopted it as a pejorative — a complete transformation that essentially retired the word's original function for the communities that coined it.

Lit and GOAT (Greatest of All Time) are examples of Gen Z/Millennial crossover terms that have become genuinely multigenerational. Sports broadcasters use GOAT unselfconsciously. Grandparents say things are lit. This is the full arc: subcultural origin → youth adoption → media exposure → mainstream absorption → multigenerational use. Once a word completes that arc, it's no longer slang in any meaningful sense — it's just vocabulary.

A brief history of youth slang

The anxiety that young people are corrupting the language is at least as old as recorded written complaints about it. Every generation invents slang; every older generation worries about it. The 1920s gave English the bee's knees, the cat's pajamas, and the bees's knees — absurdist superlatives that meant excellent or admirable. The 1950s teenagers said things were cool and groovy (the latter borrowed from jazz culture). The 1960s added far out, dig (to understand or appreciate), and bread (money). The 1980s brought rad, gnarly, and tubular from California surf and skate culture. The 1990s gave us phat, chill, and bling. The 2010s had on fleek, bae, and twerk.

What makes Gen Z slang distinctive is not that it exists — youth slang always has — but the rate at which it cycles. A 1960s hippie could use groovy for a decade before it felt dated. A Gen Z term can peak and retire in six months. This is almost entirely driven by algorithmic culture: the same mechanism that spreads a word globally in 48 hours also exposes it to mockery, parody, and overuse at the same speed. When a word becomes ubiquitous — when it appears in brand tweets and local news segments — the coolness premium that made it slang evaporates almost immediately.

This acceleration doesn't mean Gen Z slang is shallow. Some terms — rizz, slay, based, no cap — have shown real staying power because they fill genuine semantic gaps or carry nuances that existing vocabulary doesn't cover as precisely. Language that fills a gap survives; language that was purely fashionable doesn't. Frequently asked questions

Rizz means natural charm and magnetic charisma — specifically the effortless ability to attract romantic interest without really trying. Someone described as having rizz doesn't use practiced pickup lines or obvious effort; the attraction seems to flow from pure presence. The word was popularized by streamer Kai Cenat and spread massively on TikTok around 2021–2022, eventually becoming Oxford's word of the year in 2023. There's also the verb form: to "unspoken rizz" someone means to attract them without saying a word.

By search volume and cultural penetration in 2025, rizz remains one of the most recognized Gen Z terms globally, even as it moves toward Established status. Terms actively trending in 2025 include delulu (delusional in a self-aware, often humorous way), understood the assignment (performed exactly what was expected, usually as a compliment), and main character (treating yourself or someone else as the protagonist of a story). Terms that have clearly faded or been retired include on fleek (peaked around 2014–2015), yeet as a pure exclamation, salty (upset or bitter — still understood but no longer feels fresh), lowkey used as pure filler with no emphasis function, and lit as a general synonym for fun or exciting. The surest sign a word has retired: it appears in a how-to guide aimed at parents, a corporate social post, or a politician's campaign material. "No cap" means "seriously" or "I'm not lying." It comes from Black American slang where capping means to lie, boast, or exaggerate. Saying "no cap" after a statement signals that you're being completely truthful — there's no exaggeration in what you just said. The opposite, cap or capping, means lying or making things up. Example: "That was the hardest exam I've ever taken, no cap." It functions similarly to older phrases like "I'm not even joking" or "for real."
