How does Rock Paper Scissors work?

Rock Paper Scissors is a simultaneous hand game where each of the three choices beats one and loses to one: Rock crushes Scissors, Scissors cuts Paper, Paper covers Rock. When both players choose the same thing, it's a tie. This creates a perfectly balanced three-way cycle — no single choice dominates the others, making the game a fair random tiebreaker when both players choose without knowing the other's pick.

Is there a winning strategy against the computer?

In this tool, the computer picks Rock, Paper, or Scissors with equal 33.3% probability each round — it has no memory of previous rounds and uses no pattern. Against a truly random opponent, no strategy can improve your expected win rate above 33.3%. However, in real play against a human, psychology matters: people tend to repeat winning moves, throw the losing move less after a loss, and "Rock" is statistically the most common first throw. Against a human opponent, throw Paper more often to exploit Rock bias.

Uses in classrooms and decisions

Rock Paper Scissors is one of the most widely used decision-making tools in the world — from settling playground disputes to major business decisions (a 2005 Sotheby's auction between Christie's and Sotheby's was decided this way). Teachers use it to introduce probability concepts (equal 1/3 outcomes), game theory (Nash equilibrium — the optimal mixed strategy is random 1/3 each), and even evolutionary biology (lizard-lizard-rock cycles in nature).

Frequently asked questions

What are the rules of Rock Paper Scissors?
Rock Paper Scissors is a three-choice hand game where each option beats one and loses to one: Rock crushes Scissors, Scissors cuts Paper, Paper covers Rock. When both players choose the same option, it's a tie. The three-way cycle means no single choice dominates the others, making the game perfectly balanced as a fair random tiebreaker.
What is the best strategy for Rock Paper Scissors?
Against a truly random computer opponent, no strategy can improve your expected win rate above 33.3% — the Nash equilibrium is to also play randomly, one-third each. Against a human opponent, psychology and pattern recognition matter: people tend to repeat a winning move, avoid throwing the option they just lost with, and statistically throw Rock most often as their first move. Against humans, throwing Paper slightly more often exploits the Rock bias.
What is the probability of winning Rock Paper Scissors?
When both players choose randomly, the probability is exactly 1/3 (33.3%) to win, 1/3 to lose, and 1/3 to tie each round. Over many rounds against a random opponent, your win, loss, and tie counts should converge toward equal thirds — this tool tracks your running session totals so you can observe this in practice.
How is Rock Paper Scissors used to make decisions?
Rock Paper Scissors is used worldwide as a neutral, fast tiebreaker that both parties accept as fair because neither side has an inherent advantage. Famous uses include a 2005 Christie's vs. Sotheby's auction decided this way, playground disputes, sports tie-breaking, and business decisions. International RPS championships are held annually, with strategy and psychology playing real roles in competitive play.
