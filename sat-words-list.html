What Is an SAT Words List?

An SAT words list is a curated collection of vocabulary that students are likely to encounter on the SAT Reading and Writing sections. This particular list contains 400+ words drawn from the academic register the modern digital SAT favors — words you would meet in serious nonfiction, scientific writing, literary criticism, and historical sources. Each entry includes a definition, an example sentence, the word's syllable breakdown, its root language, and a difficulty rating.

This page is the static reference companion to our interactive SAT Vocabulary Words tool. Where the interactive tool generates a fresh random subset every time you click Generate and includes audio pronunciations and a one-word-at-a-time Practice Mode, this page shows the whole list at once for browsing, scanning, and printing. Use this page to read through the entire vocabulary base, then switch to the tool for active recall drills.

What makes a word "SAT-level"? It is rarely the most obscure term in the dictionary. Since the 2016 redesign, the SAT tests vocabulary in context rather than through standalone definition questions, but a strong vocabulary still matters throughout the Reading and Writing sections — particularly on Words in Context items that hinge on subtle differences between near-synonyms. A word like tenuous, candid, or substantiate is far more representative of the modern test than archaic GRE-style terms.

How to Use This List to Study

A reference list works differently from a flashcard deck. Instead of drilling a few words at a time, you should read through this list in passes, layering on understanding with each one. Here is a sequence that tends to work well for SAT preparation:

  • First pass — Easy tier. Use the Difficulty filter to show only Easy words. Read straight through. The goal is recognition, not recall — you should leave the session knowing that you have seen these words and have a rough sense of meaning.
  • Second pass — Medium tier. Filter to Medium difficulty after a few days of Easy review. Read each definition and the example sentence. Pay attention to the example: it teaches connotation and register in a way that the definition alone cannot.
  • Third pass — by root language. Use the Root filter and pick Latin, then Greek, then French. Studying a family of words with shared etymology builds pattern recognition that lets you decode unfamiliar words on test day.
  • Final pass — Hard tier. Filter to Hard and read slowly. These are the words that separate a strong score from a top score on Reading and Writing.
  • Print a focused subset. Apply any combination of filters, click Print List, and you get a clean handout of just those words — useful for offline study or for marking up on paper.

Once you have read through the list, switch to the SAT Vocabulary Words tool for active recall. The tool's Practice Mode hides definitions and asks you to recall them one word at a time, which is how you move vocabulary from passive recognition to active mastery. The two pages are designed to be used together.

Most Important SAT Root Words

The single highest-leverage move in SAT vocabulary preparation is learning Latin and Greek roots. One root unlocks dozens of words — and on test day, when you see an unfamiliar word, the root is usually what lets you guess correctly from context. Below are the highest-yield roots that recur throughout this list, each with a few representative SAT words you will find filtered under it.

  • ben- (good): benefit, benevolent, benefactor, benign, beneficiary. Recognize ben- at the start of a word and you usually have a positive connotation.
  • mal- (bad): malicious, malevolent, malign, malefactor, malady. The mirror image of ben- — a word that starts with mal- almost always carries harm or wrongdoing.
  • -dict- (to say): predict, contradict, edict, malediction, indictment, dictum. One of the most productive roots in academic English.
  • -loqu- / -loc- (to speak): eloquent, loquacious, colloquial, soliloquy, grandiloquent. Speech-related vocabulary clusters densely here.
  • -gen- (birth, origin): generate, genesis, indigenous, progeny, congenital. Often appears in science and biology passages.
  • -chron- (time): synchronize, anachronism, chronic, chronology. Frequently appears in history and literary criticism.
  • -phil- (love of): philosophy, philanthropy, bibliophile, philology. Greek-root cluster, common in humanities passages.
  • -vert- / -vers- (to turn): divert, avert, subvert, invert, introvert, conversion. A Latin workhorse.
  • -ped- (foot or child): pedantic, expedite, impede, pedestrian, pediatric. Watch context — the same root carries two meanings.
  • -script- / -scrib- (to write): prescribe, inscription, manuscript, conscription, transcribe. High-frequency on the Writing section.

Use the Root filter above to pull just the Latin or Greek words and study them as families. Spending one session per major root often produces better retention than scattering memorization across unrelated words.

SAT Words With Definitions vs SAT Vocabulary Practice

There are two complementary approaches to building SAT vocabulary, and both matter. The first is passive familiarity — reading through a full list with definitions visible, again and again, until the words become familiar. That is what this page is for. The second is active recall — quizzing yourself on the meaning of a word without seeing the definition, until you can summon the meaning on demand. That is what the SAT Vocabulary Words tool is for, especially its built-in Practice Mode.

The sequence that works best is to start with this list. Skim it. Read it. Filter it. Print it. Get to the point where every word looks familiar even if you cannot yet define it. Then switch to the interactive tool, generate small batches of 10 to 15 words, and run Practice Mode to drill recall. Save the words you miss to your saved list, and revisit them in a few days. The full list here is the broad-base study material; the tool is the precision drill. Use them in that order and the words will stick.

Frequently asked questions

How many words are on the SAT words list?
There is no single official SAT words list — the College Board stopped releasing one after the 2016 redesign. This curated list contains 400+ words commonly seen in SAT Reading passages and Writing questions on the modern digital test, drawn from the academic register the test actually favors.
Should I memorize every word on this list?
No. Focus on Easy and Medium words first to build a strong base, then use the difficulty filter to drill the Hard words last. Mastering 200 to 300 well-known words is more valuable than skimming the entire list once — depth beats breadth.
How is this different from the SAT Vocabulary Words tool?
This page shows every word at once for browsing, reading, searching, and printing. The SAT Vocabulary Words tool generates random subsets and includes audio pronunciation, saved-word lists, and a one-word-at-a-time Practice Mode for active recall. Use this page for broad review and the tool for drilling.
Can I print this list?
Yes. Click Print List, or use Ctrl/Cmd+P. The filter controls and navigation are automatically hidden in print view, leaving a clean printable study list with definitions and example sentences. Apply any filter first to print a focused subset.
What does the root note tell me?
The root note shows the word's etymology — its Latin, Greek, French, or Anglo-Saxon origin and a short note on how it got into English. Knowing the root often lets you decode unfamiliar words on test day, because many SAT words share the same prefixes and root meanings.
Does the SAT still test vocabulary directly?
Not with standalone vocabulary questions. The digital SAT tests vocabulary inside Words in Context questions throughout the Reading and Writing sections. Those questions require knowing precise word meanings and connotations, not just dictionary definitions in isolation — which is why example sentences matter.
