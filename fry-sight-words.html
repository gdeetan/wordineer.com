    What are Fry sight words?

    Fry sight words are a list of 1,000 English words compiled by Dr. Edward Fry, first published in 1957 and updated in 1980. Unlike the Dolch list, which organizes words by grade level, the Fry list orders words purely by frequency of use in written English — from most common (#1, "the") to least common (#1000). Dr. Fry based his list on analysis of millions of words from a wide range of texts, making it a reliable measure of which words appear most often in print.

    The first 100 Fry words alone account for approximately 50% of all words encountered in typical reading material. The first 300 words account for roughly 65%. This frequency weighting is the defining feature of the Fry list — learning the first 100 words gives a child a higher immediate boost to reading fluency than learning any other 100 words in the language.

    Fry vs. Dolch: key differences

    Both lists are widely used and both are public domain. Here is how they differ in practice:

    • Organization: Dolch organizes words by grade level (Pre-K, K, 1st, 2nd, 3rd). Fry organizes by frequency (1–100, 101–200, etc.). If your school uses Dolch-level groupings, use the Dolch list. If you want to prioritize by frequency regardless of grade, use Fry.
    • Scope: Dolch has 315 words (through 3rd grade). Fry has 1,000 words (through approximately 9th grade). For readers above 3rd grade, Fry is the only list that continues to offer benchmarks.
    • Overlap: The two lists share roughly 60% of their words in the K-3 range. A child who knows Dolch well will already know most of Fry 1–200.
    • Practical choice: Many teachers use both — Dolch for K-2 structured reading instruction; Fry for measuring progress through upper elementary and middle school.

    How to use this tool effectively

    1. Start with Words 1–100 if your child is in K-1 or is a beginning reader. These are the most frequent and highest-leverage words for fluency.
    2. Move to 101–200 once the first set is solid. Test mastery by running a 15-word Practice Mode session — if your child reads all 15 correctly on first sight, they're ready to advance.
    3. Use the frequency rank badge (the number next to each word) to understand priority. Lower-ranked words appear more often in text and should be mastered first.
    4. Daily 5-minute practice beats weekly long sessions. Generate 10–15 words, run through Practice Mode once, save any missed words, done.
    5. Print take-home sheets for parents to use without a device. Click Print for a clean printable word grid.

    Why frequency ordering matters

    A child who can automatically recognize the top 100 Fry words will spend significantly less mental effort decoding text, freeing up working memory for comprehension. Reading research consistently shows that fluency — the ability to read accurately and automatically without labored decoding — is one of the strongest predictors of reading comprehension. Sight word mastery is the fastest path to fluency for early readers.

    The frequency ordering also gives teachers and parents a clear progress metric. "My child knows Fry 1–150" is a more precise statement than "my child knows some sight words." Use the saved-words feature to track which words still need work and monitor progress toward the next 100-word benchmark.

    Frequently asked questions

    How many Fry sight words are there?
    Dr. Fry's complete list contains 1,000 words. This page covers the first 300 — the most important for early through mid-elementary readers. The first 100 Fry words alone appear in roughly 50% of all text.
    What is the difference between the Fry list and Dolch list?
    Fry orders 1,000 words by frequency (most common first) and extends through Grade 9. Dolch organizes 315 words by grade level (Pre-K through 3rd) with an emphasis on function words. The lists overlap heavily for the first 200 words. Most K-3 classrooms use Dolch; Fry is better for tracking progress beyond 3rd grade.
    When should a child know all 300 Fry words?
    Most reading curricula target words 1–100 by end of 1st grade, words 101–200 by end of 2nd grade, and words 201–300 by end of 3rd grade. These are benchmarks, not strict deadlines — individual children vary widely.
    How is the Fry list ordered?
    By frequency in printed English — "the" is #1 because it is the most common word in text. Words become progressively less frequent as the rank increases. Learning the lowest-ranked words first gives the fastest improvement in reading fluency.
    Can I print Fry word lists?
    Yes. Select a word set, generate the list, and click Print. The print view hides all navigation and shows only the words in a colorful grid. Use Copy All to paste into a flashcard app or word processor.
    What does Practice Mode do?
    Practice Mode shows one word at a time. Click Hear Word for audio, Next to advance, and Prev to go back. Exit Practice returns to the full list. Designed for daily 5–10 minute drills.

    Who uses this tool?

    K-3 Teachers
    Tracking student progress against Fry benchmarks, generating take-home practice lists for each student's current set, and using Practice Mode for small-group fluency drills during reading center time.
    Parents
    Running daily 5-minute sight word practice at home. The frequency rank badge helps parents understand which words matter most — start with #1–20 before worrying about #200.
    Reading Specialists
    Identifying fluency gaps by testing against specific Fry ranges, using saved-words to build a personalized review list for students who struggle with particular words across multiple sessions.
    Homeschool Educators
    Following a structured reading progression aligned with Fry benchmarks. The frequency ordering provides a natural scope-and-sequence without needing a pre-made curriculum.
