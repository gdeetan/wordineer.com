    What Are SAT Vocabulary Words?

    SAT vocabulary words are the sophisticated, academic English words that appear in the passages and questions of the SAT Reading and Writing section. The College Board redesigned the SAT in 2016 and again for the digital format in 2024, but vocabulary remains central — not as isolated fill-in-the-blank questions, but embedded in context. The exam tests whether you understand words as they function in real passages, including their connotations, nuances, and how they interact with surrounding sentences.

    The types of words that appear on the SAT fall into recognizable categories: Latin-rooted academic vocabulary (ambiguous, empirical, prevalent), Greek-rooted scientific and philosophical terms (hypothesis, paradox, pragmatic), and French-influenced words of tone and judgment (nuance, platitude, malfeasance). Understanding these root systems is not just helpful — it is the single most efficient study strategy available for the vocabulary portion of the exam.

    This tool offers 500+ curated SAT-level words, each with a plain-English definition, SAT-style example sentence, etymology badge, syllable breakdown, and audio pronunciation. Filter by difficulty and root language to study systematically rather than randomly.

    Why Root Words Matter on the SAT

    Approximately 60% of English academic vocabulary descends from Latin, and another 20–25% from Greek. This means that a student who learns the core Latin and Greek roots can decode hundreds of words they have never encountered before — including words that appear on the SAT.

    Consider the Latin root bene (well, good). Once you know it, you can decode benevolent (wishing well), benefactor (one who does good), benign (well-natured), and beneficent (doing good) — four SAT-level words from a single root. The Greek root logos (reason, word) unlocks logical, eloquent, and dozens of scientific terms. The Latin mal- (bad) prefixes malfeasance, malicious, malevolent, and malnourished — all of which carry the same threatening connotation.

    High-value roots to learn for the SAT include: bene-/mal- (good/bad), -loqu-/-dict- (to speak/say), -vid-/-vis- (to see), -aud- (to hear), -cred- (to believe), -duc- (to lead), -gen- (birth, origin), -vert- (to turn), -pon-/-pos- (to place), -plic-/-plex- (to fold, involve), -spec-/-spic- (to look, see), Greek -logy (study of), -graph- (to write), -chron- (time), -phil- (love), -phob- (fear).

    Use the Root Language filter in this tool to drill Latin words in one session and Greek words in the next. The pattern recognition built over two or three sessions unlocks far more words than memorizing individual entries ever could.

    A 6-Week SAT Vocabulary Study Schedule

    Six weeks is enough time to build a strong SAT vocabulary foundation if you study consistently rather than intensively. Fifteen to twenty minutes daily outperforms two-hour cramming sessions because vocabulary consolidates during sleep — small daily inputs produce better long-term retention.

    • Week 1 — Easy words, build the habit. Generate 20 Easy words per session. Toggle on both definitions and examples. Read each word aloud, read the definition, and read the example sentence. Save any word you couldn't immediately define. Don't worry about roots yet — just get comfortable with the words and the tool.
    • Week 2 — Latin root focus. Set Root to Latin and difficulty to Medium. Three sessions on Latin words. For each word, look at the root badge and note what the root means. After the session, try to list three words you know that share the same root. This pattern-building session is the most valuable week of the schedule.
    • Week 3 — Greek root focus. Same approach as Week 2, but filter for Greek. Focus on words with -graph-, -logy, -chron-, and -phil- roots. Compare to their Latin equivalents where they exist (Greek chronos = time; Latin tempus = time).
    • Week 4 — Medium full sweep. Remove the root filter and drill all Medium words at random. Practice Mode on a 20-word session daily. Save every word you can't immediately define. At the end of the week, your saved list is a personal weak-spot file.
    • Week 5 — Hard words. Set difficulty to Hard and generate 15 words per session. These are words with subtle connotations that the SAT uses in inference and tone questions. Focus on the example sentence — it shows how the word is used in context, which is exactly how the SAT tests it.
    • Week 6 — Saved words and synthesis. Every session: open with 10 minutes on your saved words in Practice Mode, then 10 minutes on fresh random words across all difficulty levels. On the day before your test, do only Easy and Medium words — confidence matters more than new material at this point.

    How SAT Vocabulary Is Actually Tested

    The digital SAT tests vocabulary through "Words in Context" questions on the Reading and Writing section. These questions present a short passage and ask you to choose the word or phrase that best completes the meaning of the passage.

    A typical question looks like this: a passage discusses a policy debate, uses a word with a specific nuance in context, and then asks "As used in the passage, the word 'prevalent' most nearly means…" — followed by four options: widespread, dangerous, controversial, recent. The correct answer is widespread, but a student who only vaguely knows "prevalent means something about disease or problems" might choose dangerous based on a false association.

    This is why knowing the precise definition — and especially the connotation — matters more than a rough sense of the word. The SAT's vocabulary questions are designed to distinguish students who know words from those who have merely heard them. The definitions and example sentences in this tool are written to give you that precise understanding, not just a vague sense of the word's territory.

    Pay particular attention to words with positive and negative connotations that students often confuse: sanguine (optimistic) vs. cynical (distrustful); magnanimous (generous) vs. obsequious (servilely flattering); lucid (clear) vs. verbose (wordy). These pairs frequently appear as paired options in SAT context questions.

    Sample Words by Root Group

    Learning words in root families rather than in isolation is the most efficient path to expanding SAT vocabulary. Here are examples from the most productive root families for SAT preparation.

    Latin loqu-/locu- (to speak): eloquent (speaking fluently and persuasively), loquacious (excessively talkative), grandiloquent (using pompous language), soliloquy (speaking to oneself). All of these describe manner of speech — a rich concept for SAT passage questions about authorial tone.

    Latin spec-/spic- (to look, see): perspicacious (seeing clearly, shrewd), speculate (to observe, theorize), conspicuous (clearly visible), circumspect (looking around carefully, cautious), retrospective (looking back). The common thread — vision and observation — helps you anchor unfamiliar members of this family.

    Greek path- (feeling, suffering): empathy (feeling into another's experience), apathy (without feeling), antipathy (feeling against), pathos (emotional quality that evokes pity). These appear in SAT questions about author purpose and emotional tone.

    Latin -ven-/-vent- (to come): contention (a point that comes up in argument), convention (what comes together as custom), intervention (coming between), circumvent (going around). Knowing the root turns unfamiliar compounds into decodable units.

    Frequently asked questions

    Is this tool updated for the digital SAT?
    Yes. The digital SAT (launched in 2024) tests vocabulary through "Words in Context" questions on the Reading and Writing section. The format changed from isolated fill-in-the-blank to passage-based context questions, which makes knowing precise definitions and connotations even more important. The words and definitions in this tool are selected with that context-based format in mind.
    How many SAT vocabulary words should I study?
    Quality over quantity. Knowing 300 words thoroughly — definition, connotation, part of speech, and root — is far more useful than having a vague impression of 1,000 words. The 500+ words in this tool cover the core vocabulary that appears in SAT reading passages and context questions. Start here and master this set before expanding to additional word lists.
    Should I start with easy or hard words?
    Start with Easy and Medium words. The medium-difficulty vocabulary is what appears most frequently in SAT passages — these are the words that will move your score. Hard words have higher payoff per word, but there are fewer of them on any given test. Build your Medium foundation first, then layer in Hard words in the final month before your test date.
    How do I use Practice Mode?
    Click Practice Mode to enter a focused session. Each word appears one at a time. Try to recall the definition mentally before clicking Show Definition. Use the Hear button to hear the pronunciation. Navigate with Prev and Next. Click Exit Practice to return to the full word list. Save words you struggled with using the heart icon — your saved list becomes a personalized review set for the next session.
    Can I hear the words pronounced without an account?
    Yes — click the speaker icon next to any word to hear it pronounced using your browser's built-in audio. No account, download, or subscription required. Works in Chrome, Safari, Firefox, and Edge. Audio is especially helpful for words like perspicacious, garrulous, and sanguine, where the stress pattern is not obvious from reading alone.
    How is this different from Quizlet or Magoosh?
    Quizlet requires creating or finding a deck. Magoosh is a paid subscription service. This tool is free, requires no account, and loads instantly. Every word includes etymology — the root language and root meaning — because learning root patterns is far more efficient than memorizing definitions in isolation. The Root filter lets you drill Latin or Greek words separately, building the pattern recognition that unlocks dozens of unfamiliar words on test day.
